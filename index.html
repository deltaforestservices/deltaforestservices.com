About Jeff Dacey and Delta Forest Services

Jeff Dacey holds a B.Sc. in Wildlife Ecology from the University of Maine and spent years working in some of North America's most demanding wild landscapes — radio-collaring and tracking Canada lynx in Colorado and Montana, conducting wildlife surveys and cutting trees in the interior of Alaska, working in habitat management across the West. A love for the forest and where he grew up brought him back to Vermont.

That scientific foundation and a practical working knowledge of forestry shapes everything he does in Vermont's forests today. He understands that a well-managed forest has to balance many things at once: timber value, wildlife habitat, ecosystem health, and the recreational and aesthetic qualities that make a piece of land worth spending time in.

The idea to start a business came while working in Delta Junction, Alaska and after working in Vermont for a few years, the desire to build something new from the ground up only grew. After completing the Forest Business School Six-Month Essentials program and additional business classes, he had the foundation to do it — and launched Delta Forest Services to bring that combination of ecological knowledge and practical skill to Vermont landowners.

Delta Forest Services is part of an informal network of working lands professionals who work independently but can team up to tackle larger projects.

What We Do

Services for Your Forest

Invasive Plant Control

Mechanical and chemical methods tailored to the species, scale, and conditions of your land.

  • Uprooting plants — hand pulling small stems to ripping out invasive shrubs with the mini-excavator
  • Repeated cutting to exhaust root systems as plants resprout
  • Cut-stump treatment with herbicide
  • Backpack spraying for light to moderately infested areas
  • Hydro-spraying with truck-mounted skid sprayer for heavy infestations

Forest Stand Improvement

Improving species composition, structure, and long-term value — for wildlife, timber, and the health of the forest itself.

  • Girdling — killing low quality trees to retain vertical structure and provide habitat for bats and birds
  • Mast-tree release — giving hard mast trees (oaks, hickories) and soft mast trees (cherry, apple, serviceberry) an advantage for their value to wildlife
  • Crop-tree release — improving acceptable growing stock to increase the timber value of a forest
  • Patch cuts — creating early-successional habitat or view clearing
  • Timber pruning — pruning lower branches to create higher value logs at harvest

Excavation

Using a mini-excavator to build and repair the infrastructure that makes land management possible.

  • Forest trail repair and AMP remediation
  • Log landing installation
  • Building forest roads

Low-Impact Timber Harvesting

Small-scale, careful harvesting that keeps the forest healthy and works within NRCS programs where applicable.

  • Ash salvage
  • Harvesting small woodlots
  • Combining harvest with NRCS crop- and mast-tree release for economical harvest of low-quality wood
